Traffic Study Summary

The Decatur Technology Park Data Center – Trip Generation Comparison confirms that the updated plan will generate far fewer vehicle trips than the previously approved industrial use. Study prepared by American Structurepoint, Inc.

Summary Findings

  • The new plan (Oct 1, 2025) replaces prior warehouse uses with two data centers and an electrical substation.
  • Using the ITE Trip Generation Manual, 12th Edition, the analysis shows an ~80% reduction in total trips during AM and PM peaks compared to the 2020 plan.
  • The prior 2020 traffic improvements remain valid and sufficient given the lower trip volumes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Zoning, utilities, water, traffic, noise, stormwater, visual design, economics, workforce, energy leadership, and reliability.

Land use & zoning
In most jurisdictions, data centers are classified as light industrial, high-tech industrial, or commercial uses. Sabey works closely with local planning departments to confirm zoning compatibility early in the process.
Power & energy efficiency

We collaborate directly with the utility to plan phased power delivery aligned with development. Sabey facilities are purpose-built for exceptional efficiency beyond the compute load, with strong PUE performance; sites routinely earn ENERGY STAR scores in the high 90s.

Facilities include backup generators for resiliency; fuel is stored and maintained to safety standards. In a typical year, run hours are limited.

Water & cooling

We select the most efficient cooling technology by climate and sustainability goals. For Indianapolis, Sabey is designing a closed-loop cooling system that uses virtually no water during normal operation, while maintaining acceptable energy efficiency.

Across markets, many facilities are waterless or closed-loop; where water is used, operators often leverage recycled or reclaimed sources. Independent studies in major markets show usage comparable to large office buildings.

Community rate impact & cost-sharing

We do not expect upward pressure on other customers’ rates. Sabey anticipates service under a cost-of-service tariff or a Commission-approved customer-specific contract, with all applicable riders paid by the project.

Sabey funds 100% of customer-specific and dedicated facilities and makes CIAC for incremental upstream reinforcements attributable to our load — so costs are not shifted to general ratepayers. New loads mean new revenues that help spread fixed costs.

Broad grid programs are reviewed by the Commission with public disclosure of projected rate effects. Final rate impacts, if any, are determined through utility filings and orders.

How the power agreement works
We sign a long-term service agreement. The utility owns the grid to the point of interconnection (POI); we own on the customer side. Final responsibilities are defined in CIAC and interconnection agreements.
Traffic & transportation
Data centers have relatively low daily trips once operational — mostly shift changes and routine deliveries. During construction, we coordinate schedules, routes, and traffic control with the city. Recent comparisons suggest far fewer trips than many alternative uses at this location.
Noise & environmental
We design to comply with or surpass local noise ordinances and apply industry best practices. Cooling equipment and generators use enclosures and acoustic treatments; independent reviews find typical data center sound is low-frequency and rarely violates local limits. We invest in studies and mitigation where prudent.
Stormwater & environmental protection
Developments include engineered systems — retention ponds, bioswales, permeable surfaces — to manage runoff on-site and protect waterways. We comply with environmental regulations and work to avoid impacts to wetlands, sensitive habitats, and existing trees when possible.
Community & visual considerations
Sabey designs with architectural treatments, landscaping, and screening to respect surrounding uses. Exterior lighting is shielded to reduce spill. A buffer along Camby is planned to be more substantial than previously approved buffers on this site.
Economic & workforce benefits

Data centers are significant economic engines — supporting jobs, tax revenues for essential services, and local supply chains. Mature markets have seen substantial annual local tax revenue, with support for schools and public safety. Each job can support 4–6 additional jobs across the economy; construction programs can span years.

In Quincy, WA, an expanded tax base helped fund a new high school with a performing arts center, a new city hall, and a new medical facility.

Roles are high-wage and high-skill; many do not require a four-year degree. Clean, safe environments with paths through trades training and certificates.

Schools & workforce programs

We partner with schools and community colleges to create programs that prepare students for data center roles. In Quincy and Wenatchee, CTE programs expanded with industry input and created paths into technology and trades.

Subjects like computers, networking, HVAC, and electrical are universal and apply across industries. We welcome collaboration with educators and administrators in Indianapolis.

Energy use & clean energy leadership

Digital demand grows; operators upgrade chips, servers, and processes to produce more with the same energy, improving efficiency and generating sales-tax through refresh cycles.

Digital services reduce commuting and shipping; small savings across many users add up to meaningful system-wide gains.

Sabey designs for efficiency beyond compute load. Sites deliver strong PUE and frequent ENERGY STAR scores in the high 90s. The sector partners with utilities on clean energy and grid innovations.

Backup power & reliability
Emergency generators protect service during outages. They are regulated and used sparingly for short tests and true outages. Mission-critical users need continuous power so essential services remain available.
