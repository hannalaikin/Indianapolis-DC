Frequently asked questions

A deeper look at zoning, utilities, water, traffic, noise, stormwater, visual design, economic benefits, jobs, and reliability.

Land use and zoning
In most jurisdictions data centers fit within light industrial, high tech industrial, or commercial. We work with planning staff early to confirm compatibility and any approvals that may be needed.
Power and energy efficiency

We collaborate with the utility on phased power delivery that matches the build schedule. Our facilities are engineered for efficiency so non computing energy use is kept low. Sabey sites achieve strong PUE and routinely earn ENERGY STAR scores in the high nineties.

Backup generators support resiliency. Fuel is stored and maintained under safety standards. In a typical year, run hours are limited.

Water and cooling

We select cooling technology by climate and sustainability goals. For Indianapolis we plan a closed loop system that uses virtually no water during normal operation while keeping energy use acceptable.

Across markets many facilities are waterless or closed loop. Where water is used, reclaimed or recycled sources can be used when available. Studies in major markets show water use comparable to large offices.

Community rate impact and cost sharing

We do not expect upward pressure on other customers rates. Service is under a cost of service tariff or a Commission approved customer specific contract. The project pays all applicable riders.

Sabey funds customer specific and dedicated facilities and makes contributions in aid of construction for incremental upstream work due to our load. Costs are not shifted to general customers. New load adds revenue that helps spread fixed costs.

Broad grid programs follow standard processes with public review. Final rate effects, if any, are set in Commission orders.

How the power agreement works

We sign a long term service agreement. The utility owns the grid to the point of interconnection. We own our side. Final responsibilities live in interconnection and CIAC agreements. We work closely with the utility so growth is managed in line with regulation.

Traffic and transportation
Operations have low daily trips. Most trips are shift changes and routine deliveries. During construction we coordinate schedules, routes, and control plans with the city. Recent comparisons show far fewer trips than many other uses at this location.
Noise and environmental
We design to meet or exceed local noise rules. Cooling equipment and generators use enclosures and acoustic treatments. Independent reviews find typical data center sound is low frequency and compliant. We invest in studies and mitigation where needed.
Stormwater and protection
Sites include engineered systems such as retention ponds, bioswales, and permeable surfaces to manage runoff on site and protect waterways. We follow environmental rules and work to avoid impacts to wetlands, habitats, and existing trees.
Community and visual fit
We shape architecture, landscaping, and screening to respect neighbors. Lighting is shielded to limit spill. We are designing a buffer along Camby that is more substantial than past buffers on the site.
Economic and workforce benefits

Data centers can support jobs, tax revenues, and local supply chains. Mature markets have seen strong annual tax contributions for schools and public safety. Each data center job supports additional jobs across the economy. Construction programs can span years.

In Quincy Washington an expanded tax base helped pay for a new high school with a performing arts center, a new city hall, and a new medical facility.

Jobs are high skill and high wage. Many roles do not require a four year degree. Clean and safe work environments with paths through trades, vocational programs, and certificates.

Schools and workforce programs

We partner with schools and community colleges on programs that prepare students for data center jobs. In Quincy and Wenatchee, Career and Technical Education programs expanded with industry input and created paths into technology and trades.

Topics are useful across industries. Computers, networking, HVAC, and electrical are universal skills. We are open to collaboration with educators and administrators in Indianapolis.

Energy use and clean energy leadership

Digital demand grows. Operators upgrade technology and processes to produce more with the same energy. That improves efficiency and creates sales tax from refresh cycles.

Digital services can reduce commuting and shipping. Small savings across many users add up to real system gains.

Sabey designs for efficiency beyond the compute load. Our sites deliver strong PUE and frequent ENERGY STAR scores in the high nineties. The sector also partners with utilities on clean energy and grid solutions.

Backup power and reliability
Emergency generators protect service during outages. They are regulated and used sparingly for short tests and true outages. Mission critical users need continuous power so essential services keep running.
