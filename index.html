What We're Building

A state-of-the-art data center campus on 79 acres along Kentucky Avenue—designed specifically to serve the digital infrastructure needs of the 21st century while protecting and enhancing the quality of life in Decatur Township.

Site Plan Key:
Building A: 1-story, approximately 570,300 SF, 42' height (closest to residential homes with 200' buffer)
Building B: 2-story, approximately 231,300 SF, 65' height (closest to Kentucky Avenue)

Strategic Location

79-acre site on Kentucky Avenue with direct highway access, existing industrial zoning, and available utility infrastructure—minimizing disruption to residential areas.

Professional Design

Two buildings totaling approximately 800,000 square feet with extensive landscaping, 200-foot residential buffers, and thoughtful architecture respecting the community character.

Minimal Footprint

Closed-loop air-cooled systems using virtually no water, sophisticated noise control, and 80% less traffic than typical industrial development of similar size.

Understanding Electric Rates & AES Indiana

Let's address the electricity question with complete transparency and facts.

How Electric Rates Work in Indiana

Electric rates in Indiana are regulated by the Indiana Utility Regulatory Commission (IURC), an independent state agency. No utility can change rates without IURC approval after public hearings where customers can participate.

Rate cases and major capital programs are public and decided by the IURC. This ensures that rate decisions are made transparently and in the public interest.

AES Indiana has confirmed in writing that existing customer rates are not expected to be negatively impacted per AES's plan and IURC-reviewed process.

How Data Centers Often Benefit Existing Customers

Higher Commercial Rates: Data centers pay significantly higher commercial/industrial electric rates than residential customers—often 50-100% more per kilowatt-hour.

Grid Infrastructure Investment: Large customers often fund grid upgrades (substations, transmission lines) that benefit all customers but are paid for by the large customer.

Rate Base Dilution: When infrastructure costs are spread across more total kilowatt-hours consumed, the per-unit cost for everyone can decrease.

Economic Development: Utility regulators consider economic benefits when reviewing rates—jobs, tax revenue, and community investment matter in rate cases.

AES Indiana's Track Record

AES Indiana serves over 500,000 customers across Indianapolis and surrounding areas. They have successfully served multiple data centers and large industrial customers without adverse impacts to residential rates.

AES has extensive experience integrating large commercial loads into their grid infrastructure planning and has publicly supported Indiana's growing data center industry.

The company's long-term infrastructure plan already accounts for commercial growth in the Indianapolis metro area.

The Bottom Line

Existing customer rates are not expected to be negatively impacted per AES's plan and IURC-reviewed process. Data centers like this one typically support grid improvements that benefit everyone while paying premium commercial rates. All rate decisions are made publicly by the IURC, where community members can participate and voice concerns.

Frequently Asked Questions

Comprehensive answers to common questions about the project.

What exactly is a data center?+
A data center is a secure facility that houses computer servers and networking equipment that power the internet, cloud computing, and digital services we use every day—from streaming video to online banking to medical records. Think of it as a highly secure, climate-controlled building full of computers that need to run 24/7 to keep digital services available.
Why Decatur Township?+
This location offers the perfect combination: available power infrastructure, highway access for rare equipment deliveries, existing industrial zoning, and proximity to fiber optic networks—all while maintaining appropriate distance from residential neighborhoods. The site is already zoned for industrial use and has been targeted for development for years.
Will this really not increase my electric bill?+
Existing customer rates are not expected to be negatively impacted per AES's plan and IURC-reviewed process. Data centers pay significantly higher commercial rates and often fund infrastructure improvements that benefit all customers. All rate decisions are made publicly by the Indiana Utility Regulatory Commission (IURC) after public hearings where customers can participate. AES Indiana has confirmed this project aligns with their long-term infrastructure plans. Rate cases and major capital programs are public and decided by the IURC.
How can you promise so few trips per day?+
Data centers are fundamentally different from warehouses or manufacturing. Once built and operational, they require very few people on-site (50-75 employees working in shifts) and very few deliveries (servers are installed during construction, then last 5-7 years). Third-party traffic engineering firms have studied this specific site and confirmed 50-75 daily trips at full operation—verified against actual data from similar facilities nationwide.
What about noise from cooling equipment?+
Modern data centers use sophisticated acoustic engineering specifically designed to eliminate noise impact. This includes: sound-attenuating barriers and enclosures around equipment, strategic building placement with 200-foot minimum buffers from homes, professional landscaping with dense vegetation barriers, and the closest building (Building A at 42' height) positioned toward residential areas with the lowest-noise equipment. Independent acoustic engineers have verified this facility will be inaudible from residential properties.
How does the closed-loop air-cooled system work?+
Instead of using water to cool equipment (like traditional data centers), this facility uses air-based cooling in a closed loop—similar to how your refrigerator works but on a larger scale. Air is circulated through the facility, cooled by large heat exchangers, and recirculated continuously. The system is approximately 95-99% more water-efficient than traditional water-cooled data centers. Total water usage will be less than a typical retail store of similar size.
Will this hurt my property values?+
Evidence from similar projects nationwide suggests the opposite. Well-designed data centers with extensive landscaping, low traffic, and no pollution typically maintain or increase nearby property values due to: improved infrastructure (roads, utilities), economic stability and job creation, increased tax base supporting better schools and services, and professional campus-like appearance with significant buffers. We're committed to a facility that enhances rather than detracts from the community.

With typical local rates, this generates $3-5 million annually, with approximately 50-60% going to Decatur Township schools. This funding is permanent, reliable (data centers don't close or move), and grows with the property value. Critically, this is new revenue without any increase to residential property taxes—every dollar benefits schools without costing residents anything.
